How We Classify Conflict Types

February 5, 2026 · 6 min read

Our map categorizes every US foreign action into one of four conflict types. Each type reflects a different level of military involvement and strategic approach. Understanding these categories is key to reading the map and interpreting the data behind it.

Type 1: Direct War & Occupation

This is the most severe category. It covers large-scale combat operations where US ground forces engage in prolonged warfare, invasions, or military occupations of foreign territory. These conflicts involve formal or de facto declarations of hostilities, significant troop deployments, and extended periods of territorial control.

Examples

  • War in Afghanistan (2001–2021) — The longest war in US history, involving a full-scale invasion, two decades of occupation, and counterinsurgency operations.
  • Iraq War (2003–2011) — A ground invasion that toppled the Iraqi government, followed by years of military occupation and nation-building efforts.
  • Vietnam War (1965–1973) — Massive troop deployment with over 500,000 US soldiers at peak strength.

Type 2: Direct Military Intervention

This category covers targeted military operations that fall short of full-scale war. It includes airstrikes, missile strikes, drone campaigns, naval blockades, special forces raids, and limited ground operations. These actions are typically shorter in duration and more focused in scope than Type 1 conflicts.

Examples

  • Libya Intervention (2011) — NATO-led air campaign to enforce a no-fly zone and protect civilians during the civil war.
  • Drone Campaign in Yemen — Ongoing targeted strikes against militant groups without a ground presence.
  • Somalia Airstrikes — Targeted operations against Al-Shabaab positions supporting local government forces.

Type 3: Proxy War & Armed Support

In proxy conflicts, the US does not directly fight but instead arms, funds, trains, or provides intelligence to allied forces or opposition groups. This also covers CIA covert operations designed to influence the military outcome of a conflict without overt US troop involvement.

Examples

  • Afghan Mujahideen Support (1979–1989) — Operation Cyclone: the CIA armed and financed fighters resisting the Soviet occupation.
  • Syrian Civil War (2011–present) — The US supplied weapons, training, and intelligence to select rebel groups opposing the Assad regime.
  • Contra War in Nicaragua (1981–1990) — CIA-backed Contra rebels fighting the Sandinista government.

Type 4: Political Destabilization

This is the broadest category and covers non-kinetic actions: coups, election interference, economic sanctions, diplomatic pressure campaigns, propaganda operations, and other efforts to destabilize or change a foreign government. While no shots may be fired, the consequences can be just as far-reaching.

Examples

  • 1953 Iranian Coup — CIA-orchestrated overthrow of Prime Minister Mossadegh to restore the Shah.
  • Chile (1970–1973) — Economic destabilization and covert support for the military coup against President Allende.
  • Cuba Sanctions (1962–present) — Decades of comprehensive economic embargo and diplomatic isolation.

How Types Appear on the Map

Each conflict type is represented by a distinct color on the map. When you select a date range using the timeline slider, countries are shaded according to the most severe conflict type active during that period. If a country has multiple simultaneous conflicts of different types, the highest-severity type determines its color.

You can see the legend on the left sidebar of the main map page. Clicking on any highlighted country opens a popup with details about each active conflict and a link to its full description.
